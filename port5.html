Comparative Study on Different Types of Motherboards

A comparative study of different types of motherboards shows that each form factor serves specific user needs, from powerful workstations to compact home PCs, and offers unique advantages and disadvantages based on size, expansion capability, compatibility, and typical use case.

WHAT IS A MOTHERBOARD?

The motherboard is a computer's backbone, serving as the primary platform where key components like the CPU, RAM, and expansion cards are connected. It facilitates communication and power distribution among these components and includes ports for external peripherals and storage devices.

WHAT IS A FORM FACTOR?

Each component is specifically designed to fit a certain size and design specifications in the hardware world. This is especially true in computers, where internal hardware like motherboards, storage drives, and memory modules vary widely in shape and size. This variation is referred to as the 'form factor.'

Build: Refers to the physical size, shape, and design of the motherboard, including the layout of components and the form factor (e.g., ATX, Micro ATX). It affects compatibility with PC cases and cooling solutions.

CPU Slots: The number of sockets on the motherboard where the central processing unit (CPU) is installed. Most consumer motherboards have one CPU slot; server/workstation boards may have multiple.

Memory Slots: Slots where RAM (Random Access Memory) modules are installed. The number determines how much memory can be added and supported.

Chipsets: Integrated circuits on the motherboard that manage data flow between the CPU, memory, and peripherals. They determine compatibility, supported features, and expansion capabilities.

BIOS: Basic Input/Output System firmware that initializes hardware during boot-up and provides runtime services. Modern boards use UEFI, a more advanced BIOS.

PCI Slots: Slots that allow addition of expansion cards like graphics cards, sound cards, or network cards. PCIe (PCI Express) is the current standard.

SATA: Serial ATA ports for connecting storage devices like hard drives and SSDs. They support data transfer and power.

Builtin Features: Integrated capabilities on the motherboard such as audio, LAN (network), USB ports, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, RAID controllers, and more, reducing the need for extra cards.

Motherboards come in various types and sizes, catering to different needs and specifications of computer systems. The most common types are:

AT Motherboard

If you want a PC with an extended ATX, than AT motherboard is the best choice as they have bigger physical dimensions. You cannot use them for mini desktops. Apart from that you need to add new drivers. For power connectors you need sockets and six-pin plugs. However, you cannot identify them easily and so face issues in connecting them. In the 1980s AT motherboards were used a lot. These are good for mid-range builds where you are looking for expansion rather than storage.

ATX Motherboard

ATX motherboards are the most common and versatile, supporting extensive expansion and robust performance needs. They typically feature 4–8 RAM slots, up to 7 PCIe expansion slots, multiple SATA/M.2 ports, and rich built-in connectivity like USB 3.2, Wi-Fi, and high quality sound. Their size allows for effective cooling and multiple GPUs, making them suited for gaming and demanding applications. However, they require larger cases and are more expensive than smaller types.

BTX Motherboard

BTX stands for Balanced Technology Extended. This motherboard focuses on new technologies that fulfills all power requirements. However, it results in more heat and hence Intel stopped creating BTX boards in the mid-2000s. They wanted to focus on low power CPUs.

Extended ATX (E-ATX) Motherboard

E-ATX motherboards are larger than ATX, measuring up to 12 x 13 inches or more, with support for 8+ RAM slots and multiple CPUs for workstations and servers. These boards allow for maximum expandability, best cooling, and high-end features but require big cases and tend to be costly.

LPX Motherboard

LPX boards are compact and used mainly in older systems. Featuring riser cards for expansion, they fit slim desktop cases but lack features like advanced graphics support and have been replaced by newer standards.

Micro ATX Motherboard

Micro ATX boards are smaller, usually offering up to 4 RAM slots and 4 PCIe slots. Despite their compact size, they maintain many features of ATX, remain compatible with ATX cases, and are cost-effective. Micro ATX is ideal for users prioritizing space and budget but with fewer expansion options and sometimes limited cooling potential.

Mini ITX Motherboard

Mini ITX is the smallest mainstream form factor at 6.7 x 6.7 inches. With only 2 RAM slots and one PCIe slot, Mini ITX boards focus on efficiency, low power, and compact builds for HTPCs or minimalist setups. Advanced models can support power-user features, but there’s limited expansion and airflow, and prices may be higher for high-performance ITX boards.

Mini ATX Motherboard

Mini ATX motherboard generally refers to the Micro ATX (mATX) form factor, which is a smaller and more compact version of the standard ATX motherboard, typically measuring 9.6 by 9.6 inches (244 x 244 mm). Designed to fit into smaller cases while maintaining many features of larger boards, Micro ATX supports a single CPU socket, around 2 to 4 RAM slots, and fewer PCIe expansion slots (usually up to 4) compared to standard ATX motherboards. It balances size, cost, and functionality, making it popular for budget-minded builds, office PCs, and compact gaming rigs. Despite its smaller size, it retains compatibility with most ATX components and cases, offering good performance in a more space-efficient form factor

Extended ATX (E-ATX) Motherboard

E-ATX motherboards are larger than ATX, measuring up to 12 x 13 inches or more, with support for 8+ RAM slots and multiple CPUs for workstations and servers. These boards allow for maximum expandability, best cooling, and high-end features but require big cases and tend to be costly.

Pico BTX motherboard

Pico BTX are smaller in size and the best choice if you are planning to develop a Hackintosh (desktop Mac) computer. There are two expansion slots and riser cards so as to meet all the digital application needs. If the space is your premium need, you can go for these types of computer motherboards.
